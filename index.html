Streaming Platform

Overview

More content,
less satisfaction

Streaming platforms suffer from a consistent paradox: more content, less satisfaction. The more titles a platform adds, the harder it becomes for users to find something to watch.

This project is a strategic redesign focused on the discovery experience — not the catalogue, but the interface that sits in front of it.

Core hypothesis: Users don’t have a content problem. They have a decision problem. The interface creates it — and the interface can solve it.

The Problem

7 interactions
before pressing play

I audited three major streaming platforms, documenting every friction point in a 30-minute browsing session. The patterns were brutally consistent:

Homepage rows are ordered by business logic — promoted content, partnership deals — not user intent. Search requires you to already know what you want. Content cards provide almost no information to make a decision with.

Users are forced to make high-stakes decisions (watch something for 2 hours) with almost no useful signal. The result: choice paralysis and sessions that end with nothing played.

Average interactions from open to pressing play: 7+. Target: 3.

The Approach

Three changes.
One principle.

Every decision went through the same filter: does this help the user get to something they’ll enjoy watching, faster?

Three structural changes: introducing mood-based content entry points on the homepage; redesigning the content card to surface useful metadata; and restructuring the detail page to eliminate decisions before play.

The new homepage opens with a single editorial hero — one recommendation per session with a transparent reason why. Below it, content is organised by mood rather than genre or algorithm row.

Key Decisions

The reasoning
behind the work

  1. 01
    Mood-based filtering as the primary discovery mechanism

    Mood is how users actually think — not by genre or year. Replacing the homepage scroll with 5 mood entry points narrows the decision space before browsing begins. Fewer choices equal faster, more satisfying decisions.

  2. 02
    Content cards redesigned for honest metadata

    Old cards: title + badge. New cards: title, year, runtime, and a single honest rating. Duration is critical — “do I have time for this?” was invisible. Now it’s the second piece of information shown.

  3. 03
    Play is the first interactive element on detail pages

    Unambiguous primary action, above the fold, with no competition from episode lists or related content. One clear next step.

  4. 04
    Progress indicators removed from the homepage

    “72% complete” creates anxiety on an entry surface that should feel fresh. Progress is preserved in library and detail views where it’s contextually useful.

  5. 05
    Single editorial hero with transparent reasoning

    Instead of an autoplay hero promoting a platform deal, one curated recommendation with a transparent reason: “Because you watched X.” Signal, not noise.

Outcome

Fewer steps.
Better decisions.

The redesign shifts the fundamental question of the interface. The old homepage asked: “What do you want to watch?” The new one asks: “What are you in the mood for right now?”

That single reframe changes everything downstream — layout, card design, search model, detail hierarchy. A consistent principle produces a consistent interface.
