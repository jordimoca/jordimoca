Healthcare Interfaces:
Lessons from the Field

Why medical software has uniquely hard UX constraints — and the principles I’ve developed working on prescription workflows, clinical observation systems and medication management tools.

Contents

Why healthcare UI is different

Healthcare software is one of the few design contexts where getting it wrong has direct, measurable consequences for real people. A confusing prescription interface doesn’t just cause frustration — it can cause medication errors. A poorly designed observation system can mean a clinician misses a critical alert. The stakes create constraints that most consumer UX design doesn’t have to account for.

I’ve spent a significant part of my career working on medical software: prescription workflows, dosage management, clinical observation systems and medication administration tools. These are not glamorous UX problems. They don’t generate case studies about beautiful animations or delightful onboarding. But they’re among the most demanding design problems I’ve worked on, and the lessons translate directly to any complex, high-stakes interface.

“In healthcare UX, the question isn’t ‘how do we make this enjoyable?’ It’s ‘how do we make this impossible to get wrong?’ Those are very different design problems.”

The three constraints that shape everything

Every design decision on a healthcare product has to be filtered through three constraints that don’t apply with the same force anywhere else. Understanding these constraints is more important than understanding any specific design pattern.

  1. Regulatory compliance — Healthcare software operates under legal and clinical governance frameworks. The UX must work within, not against, these requirements. Data display formats, confirmation flows and error handling are often constrained by compliance requirements that predate the design work.
  2. Clinical workflow integration — Healthcare software is never used in isolation. It sits inside a complex clinical workflow that involves other systems, physical processes and professional protocols. A brilliant standalone UX that doesn’t fit the workflow will be rejected or worked around.
  3. Error cost asymmetry — In most products, errors are recoverable. In healthcare, some errors are not. This creates an asymmetry in design priorities: preventing the wrong action matters more than streamlining the right one. Confirmation dialogs that would be eliminated as friction in a consumer app are necessary here.

Designing for power users, not beginners

Most consumer products optimise heavily for the new user experience. Onboarding flows, tooltips, empty states — a disproportionate amount of design effort goes into the first few sessions. Healthcare software has the opposite problem.

A nurse using a medication administration system will interact with it dozens of times per shift, hundreds of times per week. A prescribing clinician will navigate the prescription workflow multiple times every working day. These are not beginner users. They are expert users who have already paid the learning cost, and who will pay a daily tax for every piece of unnecessary friction in the interface.

This changes the design priorities fundamentally:

  • Keyboard navigation is a feature, not a nice-to-have. Expert users route around mouse-based interactions wherever possible. If your form can’t be navigated entirely by keyboard, you’ve added friction for every power user, every time.
  • Defaults should reflect the 80% case. Pre-populating fields based on clinical context (patient age, existing medications, department norms) reduces the cognitive overhead of the most common tasks.
  • Speed is a clinical outcome, not a preference. If a prescription workflow takes 4 minutes instead of 2, that’s not just user frustration — it’s two minutes of a clinician’s time that isn’t spent on clinical care.

Information hierarchy in clinical workflows

Clinical interfaces deal with dense, complex information. A prescription form might need to show patient demographics, current medications, allergies, contraindications, dosage options, route of administration, frequency, duration and prescriber notes — simultaneously, in a way that supports a fast, accurate decision.

Getting information hierarchy right in this context requires making deliberate editorial decisions about what the clinician needs right now versus what they need occasionally. The failure mode is to show everything all the time, which means nothing stands out when it needs to.

Progressive disclosure in clinical contexts

Progressive disclosure — showing basic information by default and revealing additional detail on demand — is a well-established UX pattern. In healthcare it requires careful calibration. Hiding information that a clinician should see for safety is dangerous. Showing all information at once creates noise that makes it harder to see the important things.

The rule I’ve developed is: safety-critical information is always visible; decision-support information is surfaced on demand. Allergy alerts, active contraindications, critical lab values — these are always on screen. Extended drug information, historical dosage records, cross-reference data — these can live behind a single interaction.

What I learned building prescription workflows

The prescription workflow is one of the most complex and highest-stakes flows in clinical software. A clinician is making a decision that directly affects a patient’s medication, dosage and schedule. The interface has to support that decision without getting in the way, while also preventing errors that could cause harm.

Several specific lessons from this work:

  1. The order of fields matters more than which fields you include. Clinicians think about prescribing in a specific sequence: drug name → indication → dose → route → frequency. The form should reflect that sequence. Anything that breaks the clinical mental model creates friction and increases error risk.
  2. Inline validation is more dangerous than end-of-form validation in some contexts. In a prescription form, showing a dosage error inline can interrupt a clinician’s working memory at a critical moment. Contextual errors should appear after the field is complete, not as the user types.
  3. Confirmation steps must earn their place. Every extra confirmation adds friction. But for high-risk actions — high-alert medications, significant dose changes, override of a clinical alert — a confirmation step is a safety net, not a barrier. The design challenge is ensuring that routine confirmations don’t train users to click through without reading.

“Alert fatigue is a real clinical safety problem. When clinicians see dozens of alerts per shift, they stop reading them. Every unnecessary alert you add makes the necessary ones less visible.”

Key principles for healthcare interface design

These principles have emerged from years of working on clinical software. They apply most directly to healthcare, but most of them translate to any high-stakes, expert-user interface.

01
Safety-critical information is never hidden

Allergy alerts, active contraindications, override flags — these are always visible, never behind a click, never deprioritised by the visual hierarchy.

02
Design for the expert, accommodate the beginner

The daily experience of the expert user should not be compromised by supporting features that only matter in the first few sessions. Onboarding and help systems should be additive, not baked into the core workflow.

03
Match the clinical mental model

The interface should reflect how clinicians already think about the task. Imposing a new mental model means extra cognitive load every time the system is used.

04
Minimise alert volume, maximise alert relevance

Alert fatigue is a documented clinical safety problem. Every alert you show should be necessary. Every unnecessary alert makes the important ones harder to see.

05
Error prevention over error recovery

Unlike most consumer software, some errors in healthcare are not easily recovered from. The design priority is making the wrong action difficult or impossible, not just easy to undo.
