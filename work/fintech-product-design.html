01. Context

70% cart abandonment.
One root cause.

E-commerce checkout flows are among the most studied UX problems in the field — and among the most consistently misdesigned. Industry average cart abandonment sits at 70%+. Most of that loss happens during checkout, not before it.

The common culprits are well-documented: too many steps, too many required fields, forced account creation, surprise costs revealed at the final stage. This case study takes a structured approach to eliminating each one through research-led design decisions.

Design target: Steps 4→2. Required fields 14→8. Guest checkout as the primary flow, with account creation as an optional post-purchase step.

02. Problem

Fourteen fields.
Four steps.
Zero patience.

The checkout flow being redesigned had accumulated complexity over several product iterations. Each step had been justified individually but the cumulative effect was a checkout that asked users to make more than 20 discrete interactions before their order was confirmed.

  1. *
    Forced account creation as a gate

    Account creation was mandatory before purchase. Users who wanted to buy quickly had no path that didn’t require creating and confirming an account first.

  2. *
    Four separate steps with full-page transitions

    Cart review → shipping details → payment → confirmation. Each transition felt like starting again. Progress indicators existed but didn’t reduce the cognitive weight.

  3. *
    14 required fields, many non-essential

    Phone number, date of birth, newsletter opt-in (pre-checked) and a marketing preference question were all required. No field had been audited for necessity since the initial build.

  4. *
    Delivery cost revealed on the final screen

    Users regularly abandoned at step 4 after encountering an unexpected delivery cost. The cost was not shown until after entering all their details.

03. Research

Understanding
where users leave

I combined quantitative funnel data with qualitative session recordings and exit interviews. The funnel data showed where users abandoned; the sessions and interviews showed why.

  1. 1
    47% abandoned at account creation

    The account gate was the single largest drop-off point. Nearly half of users who reached the checkout left when presented with the registration requirement.

  2. 2
    22% abandoned at the delivery cost reveal

    Exit surveys confirmed the delivery cost surprise. “I would have bought it if I’d known the shipping before I started” was the most common verbatim response.

  3. 3
    Form fatigue between steps 2 and 3

    Session recordings showed users slowing down significantly between shipping and payment. Re-entering payment details that their browser had already auto-filled was a noted frustration.

04. Design Process

Audit every field.
Justify or remove.

The redesign process began with a field-level audit. Every required field was mapped against two questions: does the business genuinely need this? and does it need to be collected at this point? Fields that failed either test were removed or deferred.

  1. 1
    Guest checkout as the default, always

    Account creation becomes a post-purchase offer: “Save your details for next time?” Users who want accounts still get them — after they’ve bought, not before. The gate is removed entirely.

  2. 2
    Shipping and payment on one screen

    The four-step flow collapses to two: cart review and checkout. Shipping and payment are on a single screen, with real-time delivery cost shown as soon as a postcode is entered.

  3. 3
    Field reduction: 14 to 8

    Phone number (moved to optional), date of birth (removed), newsletter opt-in (moved to account creation post-purchase), marketing preference (removed from checkout). Each removal justified against business data.

  4. 4
    Delivery cost transparency from the cart

    Estimated delivery cost is shown on the cart page before checkout begins. No surprises. Users enter the checkout informed.

05. Key Decisions

Why each change
was worth making

  1. 01
    Guest-first is not a small change

    It requires rethinking how account-linked features (order history, saved addresses, loyalty points) work. The post-purchase account offer is the bridge — users who want those features still get them, but the purchase path is cleared.

  2. 02
    Real-time delivery cost requires backend support

    Postcode-based delivery calculation needs to be fast enough to feel instant. If the API call takes more than 300ms, users notice. This was a significant engineering constraint that shaped the design.

  3. 03
    Form consolidation is a trust question, not just a UX one

    Putting shipping and payment on one screen can feel overwhelming if not designed carefully. The layout uses clear visual separation between the two sections, with a persistent order summary to maintain context.
